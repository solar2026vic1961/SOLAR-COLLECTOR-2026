DOUBLE-CIRCUIT SOLAR COLLECTOR WITH WATER-BASED IR BACK-RADIATION FILTER
(Prior art disclosure, public domain)

Date of publication: 2026-06-08
Author: anonymous. This document is released into the public domain (CC0).

1. FIELD OF APPLICATION
The device relates to solar collectors for heating water to high temperatures
(well above ambient) with minimal heat losses.

2. BACKGROUND
Flat-plate and vacuum tube collectors with selective coatings are known.
Their drawbacks: complex manufacturing, high cost, and IR radiative losses
when heated above 60–70°C.

3. DISCLOSURE OF THE INVENTION

3.1. Principle of operation (simplified)
Liquid water has a unique optical property: it is transparent to visible
(short-wave) radiation in the 0.4–0.75 µm range, but strongly absorbs
infrared (IR, long-wave) radiation emitted by hot objects.

The proposed design uses this property as follows:
— Cold water, taken from a water source, flows through the outer circuit
  and freely transmits sunlight to the black absorber in the inner circuit
  (due to water transparency in the visible range).
— The same cold water simultaneously acts as a filter, trapping the thermal
  IR radiation from the hot elements of the inner circuit and preventing it
  from escaping (due to water absorption in the IR range).
— The cold water in the outer circuit also acts as a **thermal barrier**:
  it keeps the outer wall of the collector cool, so that radial conductive
  losses from the hot core are virtually absent (no significant temperature
  difference between the outer wall and the ambient air).
— The absorbed heat (from both back-radiation and any conductive flows)
  remains in the system and is returned to the heat transfer fluid.

3.2. Construction
— Inner circuit: a transparent tube (glass or polycarbonate) containing
  a black matte absorber heated by solar radiation. The heated water flows
  inside this tube.
— Outer circuit: a 1–2 mm gap between two transparent tubes (outer and inner)
  through which cold water from a water source flows (the same water that
  later enters the inner circuit).
— An additional sealed gap between the two transparent tubes may be filled
  with air, carbon dioxide, argon, or another gas of low thermal conductivity.
  The thickness of this gap must be small (a few millimetres) to suppress
  convection.
— The black absorber is placed INSIDE the transparent inner tube, so that
  heat is transferred directly to the water at the contact zone, not through
  the tube wall.

3.3. Detailed description of processes (step by step)

Step 1. Transmission of sunlight.
Solar radiation passes through the outer transparent wall, then through the
cold water layer in the outer gap (water is transparent to visible radiation
in the 0.4–0.75 µm range), then through the wall of the inner tube, and finally
strikes the black absorber, heating it.

Step 2. Heating of water in the inner circuit.
Water flowing inside the inner tube directly contacts the black absorber
and takes up heat without intermediate layers. The water temperature rises.

Step 3. Backward thermal IR radiation.
The hot elements of the inner circuit (water, tube wall, black absorber)
emit thermal radiation in the infrared range (long-wave IR). This radiation
is almost completely blocked by the cold water layer in the outer gap:
water strongly absorbs IR radiation. The radiation is absorbed by the outer
water layer, heating it.

Step 4. Blocking of conductive losses.
Because the outer water layer remains cool (it is continuously replaced by
fresh water from the source), the temperature of the collector's outer wall
is close to ambient. The temperature gradient "hot core — outside air" is
interrupted: heat that would otherwise escape through the tube wall and
gas gap is intercepted by the cold water of the outer circuit.

Step 5. Return of heat to the system.
The water in the outer gap, warmed by absorbing back IR radiation and
intercepted conductive flows, then enters the inner circuit and is further
heated to the final temperature. Thus, both radiative and conductive losses
that would be irretrievably lost in conventional designs are returned to the
heat transfer fluid.

4. ENERGY BALANCE (estimated, per 1 m²)
Solar radiation under clear skies: about 1000 W/m².
Of this:
— about 830 W falls in the visible and near-IR range where water is
  transparent; this energy reaches the black absorber.
— about 170 W is absorbed directly by the outer water layer (this is also
  useful heat).

Thermal IR emission of water heated to a high temperature (e.g., at 90°C)
is about 520 W/m². In the proposed design, this emission is completely
absorbed by an outer water layer only 1–2 mm thick and does not escape the
system.

Result: radiative and conductive losses are virtually zero.

5. DISTINCTIVE FEATURES
— The same water is used as the heat transfer fluid in two sequential circuits.
— A 1–2 mm cold water layer (taken from a water source) acts as a selective
  IR filter, absorbing the backward thermal radiation from the hot elements
  of the inner circuit.
— The same 1–2 mm cold water layer simultaneously acts as a thermal barrier,
  blocking radial conductive losses from the hot core (the outer wall
  temperature remains close to ambient).
— The black absorber is placed INSIDE the transparent inner tube, preventing
  heat loss through the tube wall and ensuring direct contact between water
  and absorber.
— A thin gas gap (a few millimetres) between the transparent tubes reduces
  heat conduction and suppresses convection.

6. ADVANTAGES
— Simple construction from readily available materials (polycarbonate tubes,
  black paint).
— No expensive vacuum chambers or selective coatings.
— Radiative and conductive losses are minimised by exploiting the inherent
  optical and thermal properties of water.
— The outer cold water shell remains cool, reducing conductive losses to the
  environment.
